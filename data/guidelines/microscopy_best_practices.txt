# Blood Smear Microscopy Best Practices for Malaria Diagnosis
# Source: WHO Malaria Microscopy Quality Assurance Manual, CDC DPDx

## Thin Blood Smear
- Shows individual RBC morphology — best for species identification
- Fixed with methanol, stained with Giemsa (3% for 30-45 min or 10% for 10-15 min)
- Examine under 100x oil immersion
- Count parasitemia: % of infected RBCs per 500-2000 RBCs
- Can identify: ring forms, trophozoites, schizonts, gametocytes

## Thick Blood Smear
- More sensitive — concentrates parasites (10-20x more than thin film)
- Not fixed (RBCs are lysed) — parasites seen against clear background
- Count parasites per 200-500 white blood cells
- Better for detecting low parasitemia
- Harder to determine species

## Common Artifacts That Mimic Parasites
- Platelets overlying RBCs (most common false positive)
- Stain precipitate / debris
- Howell-Jolly bodies (nuclear remnants)
- Iron inclusion bodies (Pappenheimer bodies)
- Bacteria in contaminated samples
- Poor staining technique

## Quality Control in AI-Assisted Microscopy
- AI models trained on well-stained Giemsa samples may underperform on poorly stained slides
- Lighting conditions during image capture affect model accuracy
- Field-prepared slides have more variability than lab-prepared reference standards
- CNN models should be validated on images from the target population and setting
- Regular recalibration against expert microscopist readings recommended

## Parasitemia Estimation
- Method: Count infected RBCs per 500-2000 total RBCs on thin film
- Formula: (Infected RBCs / Total RBCs counted) × 100 = % parasitemia
- Low: <1% (favorable prognosis, oral treatment usually sufficient)
- Moderate: 1-5% (close monitoring, consider admission)
- High: >5% (severe malaria, IV artesunate, ICU care)
- Hyperparasitemia (>10%): Very high mortality risk, exchange transfusion may be considered

## When AI Confidence Is Low
- If AI model confidence is below 70%, recommend:
  1. Verify image quality (focus, staining, lighting)
  2. Capture additional images from different fields
  3. Request expert microscopist review
  4. Consider RDT as complementary test
  5. Treat clinically if symptoms are suggestive (don't wait for confirmation in severe cases)
